Executive Summary

I am completing a Master of Science in Applied Data Science at Syracuse University's School of Information Studies, in collaboration with the Whitman School of Management. Before enrolling, I spent nearly two decades in higher education student success — advising undergraduate students at Penn State, Miami University, and Syracuse University.

"I didn't leave education to do data science. I brought data science back to it."

I wanted to understand the data behind the patterns I had been observing in students for years, and to develop the analytical tools to turn institutional observation into evidence that drives change.

What I have come to believe: the most important skill in applied data science is not any single method. It is the ability to ask a question that matters, apply rigorous methods honestly, and communicate findings to people who can act on them.

Business Need

In Spring 2024, ECS piloted "Powering Through Academic Recovery" — a mandatory workshop replacing the formal appeal process for students on the highest level of probation. Held January 26, 2024, it was grounded in design thinking: mind mapping, problem framing, prototyping, and articulating NACE career competencies. This project measures whether it actually worked.

What I Built

A text mining pipeline on real pre/post workshop survey responses to determine whether students' language and sentiment shifted after the workshop. KMeans clustering, TF-IDF, PCA, VADER sentiment analysis.

Key Finding

KMeans separated Pre and Post responses without using labels during training — language patterns shifted enough to be distinguishable by content alone. Pre: 'problem,' 'help,' 'plan.' Post: 'mind map,' 'enjoy,' 'connections.' First quantitative analysis ever conducted on this workshop's feedback.

Learning Outcome

I had run this workshop and believed it was working — but believing something and being able to show it are very different things. What surprised me most was that the algorithm confirmed what I had sensed in the room: students weren't just saying different things after the workshop. They were thinking differently.

Business Need

Does EV adoption actually reduce emissions — or does grid composition determine the outcome? Policymakers assuming EV adoption alone drives decarbonization may be missing the bigger picture. This project tests that assumption across 8 world regions and 272 years of data.

What I Built

A multi-source analysis merging IEA Global EV Outlook, Kaggle EV sales data, emissions datasets, and the Global Carbon Budget to examine EV adoption vs. CO₂ emissions across regions and income groups.

Key Finding

Asia leads in EV adoption (18.5M sales) yet emissions remain high — the grid is still coal-dependent. More EVs ≠ fewer emissions without grid decarbonization. Battery Electric Vehicles dominate all powertrain types globally, with exponential growth since 2016.

Learning Outcome

This project taught me to be skeptical of optimistic headlines. Good data science doesn't confirm what we want to believe — it asks harder questions. The data tells a more complicated story than 'EVs are saving the planet.'

Business Need

Student success is shaped by far more than GPA and attendance. As Schreiner (2012) argues, student success encompasses academic achievement, engagement, satisfaction, and personal development. Traditional metrics provide only a partial picture. This study analyzes 2,392 students to identify key predictors and offer actionable insights that extend beyond grades.

What I Built

A full comparison across 9 data mining methods: association rule mining, K-Means and hierarchical clustering, regression, decision trees, Naive Bayes, SVM, kNN, and Random Forest on a dataset of 2,392 high school students.

Key Finding

Absence–GPA correlation: 0.92 — each additional absence = 0.92-point GPA drop. SVM and Random Forest achieved ~78–80% accuracy. Highest-lift rule (6.73): high GPA + very low absences + tutoring → B outcome — directly translatable to advising practice.

Learning Outcome

Twenty years of advising taught me that the students who disappear rarely announce it. This project confirmed what I already knew in my gut — absences matter more than almost anything else — and gave me the statistical language to say it with precision.

Business Need

In clinical AI, class imbalance is critical: a model predicting the majority class for every patient may achieve high accuracy while failing to identify the cases that matter most. For thyroid cancer risk, missing a malignant case carries far greater consequences than a false positive. This project addresses how to build a model that is both accurate and trustworthy.

What I Built

A clinical risk classification model on 10,558 US patient records, addressing class imbalance through PCA, SMOTE, class weighting, and interaction terms. Compared Gradient Boosting, SVM, Logistic Regression, and Random Forest.

Key Finding

Gradient Boosting: 79.73% accuracy. SVM: strongest recall for malignant cases — the clinically important metric. PCA-composite features outperformed raw clinical indicators, raising a critical question: a model that is accurate but opaque may not be trustworthy enough for clinical use.

Learning Outcome

The hardest moment in this project was realizing that the most accurate model was also the least explainable. If a physician cannot understand why the model flagged a patient, the model is not truly useful, no matter how high its accuracy. This changed how I think about what it means to build something trustworthy.

Business Need

Dietary tracking applications require accurate image classification across hundreds of categories — while also detecting inputs outside the model's training distribution. Standard classifiers fail gracefully when encountering unknown categories. This project addresses both challenges using a hybrid deep learning architecture.

What I Built

A hybrid framework: MobileNetV2 transfer learning classifier (95.2% accuracy on 90+ categories) combined with a convolutional autoencoder for anomaly detection via elevated reconstruction error on out-of-distribution inputs.

Key Finding

The autoencoder, trained only on 'healthy' foods, produced statistically distinguishable reconstruction errors for unknown inputs. Key insight: anomaly detection is portable across domains — in student data, an anomaly might be a sudden drop in LMS logins or a divergent grade trajectory.

Learning Outcome

What I took from this project wasn't really about food. It was about learning to build systems that know what they don't know. The autoencoder flagged images it had never seen before — by recognizing its own uncertainty. I keep thinking about how powerful that would be in student data.

Business Need

Advisors in ECS currently rely on reactive outreach — reaching students after they appear in crisis rather than before. No predictive system exists to identify which students are most at risk of leaving before completing their degree. This independent project builds that system: an interpretable retention risk model with an advisor-facing early alert tool.

What I'm Building

Logistic Regression + Random Forest with k-fold cross-validation and SHAP values to produce interpretable risk scores. Variables: GPA, credits, advising visits, financial holds, LMS logins, Pell eligibility, first-gen status, major, probation type.

Key Design Decision

SHAP values make the model actionable: for any student, an advisor can see exactly which features are driving the risk score. A model that cannot explain itself is not useful in practice. The goal is to augment advisor judgment — not replace it.

Learning Outcome

This is the project I came to this program to build. Everything before it was preparation. What I want an advisor to be able to say is not "the model says this student is at risk." I want them to say "I reached out on Tuesday because I saw something shift in their data — and they're still here." That is the version of data science I am trying to build.
